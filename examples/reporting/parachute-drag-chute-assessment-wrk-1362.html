Scope

Objective: Determine parachute drag forces for all applicable load cases (single and dual chute configurations) per Stroud Safety sizing charts for a 3,600 lb GT1R R35. Identify the governing load case for frame design.

Inclusions

  • Single chute drag at 200 and 250 MPH
  • Dual chute drag at 250 MPH
  • Stroud sizing chart logic (single vs dual threshold)
  • Aerodynamic lift and tire traction sensitivity at deployment speeds

Methodology

Parachute drag force is calculated using the standard aerodynamic drag equation augmented with an opening shock factor (Cx) to capture the transient snatch load at deployment. Three load cases are assessed per the WRK-1362 specification: - **Case 1**: Single chute at 200 MPH (NHRA threshold) - **Case 2**: Single chute at 250 MPH (design maximum) - **Case 3**: Dual chutes at 250 MPH (Stroud recommendation for 3,600 lbs) The governing case (maximum drag force) drives the frame design load. A sensitivity analysis assesses aerodynamic lift and tire traction capacity at deployment speeds to determine whether the tires can resist the chute drag through friction or whether the frame mounts carry the full load in tension. Stroud Safety sizing charts determine the single vs. dual chute threshold (200 MPH per NHRA rules) and recommend specific chute models by vehicle weight and speed.

Standard: NHRA Safety Rules; Stroud Safety Parachute Sizing Chart (2024)

Equation 1: Parachute Drag Force
$$F = \frac{1}{2} \rho V^2 C_d A C_x$$
Drag force including opening shock factor for transient snatch load.
(1)
  • $F$ — Drag force at deployment [lbs]
  • $\rho$ — Air density [slug/ft³]
  • $V$ — Vehicle speed at deployment [ft/s]
  • $C_d$ — Parachute drag coefficient [-]
  • $A$ — Projected canopy area = π(d/2)² [ft²]
  • $C_x$ — Opening shock factor [-]
Equation 2: Dual Chute Total Drag
$$F_{dual} = F_1 + F_2 = \frac{1}{2} \rho V^2 C_x \left( C_{d1} A_1 + C_{d2} A_2 \right)$$
Sum of drag from two independently parameterized chutes.
(2)
  • $F_{dual}$ — Total drag from both chutes [lbs]
  • $F_1, F_2$ — Individual chute drag forces [lbs]
Equation 3: Aerodynamic Lift
$$F_{lift} = \frac{1}{2} \rho V^2 C_L A_f$$
Body lift force reducing effective tire normal load.
(3)
  • $F_{lift}$ — Aerodynamic lift on vehicle body [lbs]
  • $C_L$ — Vehicle lift coefficient [-]
  • $A_f$ — Vehicle frontal area [ft²]
Equation 4: Effective Tire Normal Force
$$N_{eff} = W - F_{lift} + F_{downforce}$$
Net vertical load on tires at deployment speed.
(4)
  • $N_{eff}$ — Effective normal force on tires [lbs]
  • $W$ — Vehicle weight [lbs]
  • $F_{downforce}$ — Aerodynamic downforce (0 for baseline) [lbs]
Equation 5: Available Friction
$$F_{friction} = \mu \times N_{eff}$$
Maximum longitudinal force tires can resist through friction.
(5)
  • $\mu$ — Tire–surface friction coefficient [-]
Equation 6: Speed Conversion
$$V_{fps} = V_{mph} \times \frac{5280}{3600}$$
Miles per hour to feet per second.
(6)

Calculations

Step 1: Convert deployment speeds to ft/s

V₁ = 200 × 5280/3600 = 293.33 fps; V₂ = 250 × 5280/3600 = 366.67 fps

Step 2: Compute canopy projected areas

A_single = π(6)² = 113.10 ft²; A_dual_each = π(5)² = 78.54 ft²

Step 3: Dynamic pressure at each speed

q₁ = 0.5 × 0.002378 × 293.33² = 102.27 psf; q₂ = 0.5 × 0.002378 × 366.67² = 159.79 psf

Step 4: Case 1 — single chute at 200 MPH

F = 102.27 × 1.4 × 113.10 × 1.5 = 24,289 lbs
Ref: Eq. 1

Step 5: Case 2 — single chute at 250 MPH

F = 159.79 × 1.4 × 113.10 × 1.5 = 37,952 lbs
Ref: Eq. 1

Step 6: Case 3 — dual chutes at 250 MPH

F_each = 159.79 × 1.4 × 78.54 × 1.5 = 26,356 lbs; F_total = 2 × 26,356 = 52,712 lbs
Ref: Eq. 2

Step 7: Identify governing case

Case 3 (dual @ 250 MPH) = 52,712 lbs governs

Step 8: Aerodynamic lift at deployment speeds

F_lift(200) = 102.27 × 0.35 × 25.0 = 895 lbs; F_lift(250) = 159.79 × 0.35 × 25.0 = 1,398 lbs
Ref: Eq. 3

Step 9: Tire traction capacity

N_eff(200) = 3600 − 895 = 2,705 lbs → friction = 0.8 × 2705 = 2,164 lbs; N_eff(250) = 3600 − 1398 = 2,202 lbs → friction = 0.8 × 2202 = 1,762 lbs
Ref: Eq. 4, 5

Step 10: Drag vs. friction assessment

All cases: F_drag >> F_friction. Frame mounts carry full chute load as tension/shear. Tires cannot resist deployment loads through friction alone.

Assumptions
  1. Linear aerodynamic drag model valid at deployment speeds (no compressibility)
  2. Opening shock factor Cx = 1.5 captures peak transient snatch load
  3. Both dual chutes deploy simultaneously with equal drag
  4. Sea-level air density (ρ = 0.002378 slug/ft³) — conservative for elevated tracks
  5. Vehicle body lift coefficient Cl = 0.35 estimated; no GT1R-specific aero data
  6. Zero aerodynamic downforce in baseline (splitter/wing contribution TBD)
  7. Tire friction coefficient μ = 0.8 for drag radials on prepped surface
  8. Frame attachment points are rigid relative to parachute deployment timescale
