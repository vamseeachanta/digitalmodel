1  Objective

Establish a known-answer verification for the digitalmodel OpenFOAM CFD capability by reproducing the canonical laminar boundary layer over a sharp-leading-edge flat plate and comparing the computed solution against the exact Blasius similarity solution. This is the foundational trust anchor of the CFD validation hierarchy: no domain workflow is considered production-ready until it reproduces a result with a known analytical answer. CFD without verification is decoration.

What this gates in real work

The laminar flat plate is the skin-friction foundation: every hull, pontoon and appendage drag estimate decomposes into friction + pressure components, and the friction part is a boundary-layer computation exactly like this one. Verifying Cf(x) to ~5% against an exact solution qualifies:

  • Model-scale friction lines — towing-tank extrapolation (ITTC-1957) assumes the CFD can reproduce a flat-plate boundary layer before any hull-form work is trusted.
  • Laminar-regime components — small appendages, model-scale foils and instrument struts at low Re where the laminar solution applies directly.
  • Mesh/BC discipline — the case demonstrated that leading-edge treatment (not mesh count) controls the error, a transferable lesson for any wall-bounded mesh in the suite.

2  Reference solution — Blasius (1908)

For a steady, incompressible, zero-pressure-gradient laminar boundary layer, the Prandtl boundary-layer equations admit a self-similar solution in the variable η = y√(U∞/νx). The stream-function profile f(η) satisfies the Blasius ODE:

f''' + ½ f f'' = 0,  f(0)=f'(0)=0,  f'(∞)=1

solved here by shooting with the exact wall curvature f''(0) = 0.33206. Two closed-form consequences are used as acceptance metrics:

u/U∞ = f'(η)  (velocity profile)   Cf(Rex) = 0.664 / √Rex  (local skin friction)

4  Solution verification — isolating the error sources

A first naive setup over-predicted skin friction by ~30%. Rather than accept or hand-tune the result, each candidate error source was isolated in turn — the disciplined core of a CFD verification study:

#Model changeCf error vs BlasiusInference
1Baseline: H=0.04 m, slip top, plate at inlet+28% to +33%—
2Taller domain H=0.10 m+21% to +33%blockage minor
3Entraining top BC+18% to +33%top-BC minor
4Upstream symmetry run-in (clean LE)+3% to +5%leading-edge treatment dominant
Engineering finding. The dominant error was not mesh density or domain size but the leading-edge boundary condition: placing the sharp no-slip plate directly at the velocity inlet creates a spurious singularity and a thinner-than-Blasius boundary layer. Introducing a 20 mm shear-free (symmetry) run-in ahead of the plate lets the stream arrive uniformly and the boundary layer develop from a true x=0 origin — collapsing the error from ~30% to ~5%.

6  Error attribution

The residual ~3–5% skin-friction excess is consistent with the remaining ~2.5% freestream over-speed at the boundary-layer edge (max u/U∞ ≈ 1.025): since Cf ∝ Uedge2, a 2.5% velocity excess inflates Cf by ~5%, fully accounting for the offset. This residual displacement-blockage scales with δ*/H and would reduce further with a taller domain; it is within the 5% acceptance band adopted for laminar-verification work and does not affect the qualitative profile collapse.
