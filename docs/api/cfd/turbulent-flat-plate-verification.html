1  Objective

Verify the digitalmodel CFD pipeline for turbulent flow — the regime of essentially all real engineering CFD, and the first turbulent case in the validation suite (the flat-plate Blasius and cylinder Re=100 cases are laminar). The test is the zero-pressure-gradient turbulent boundary layer, whose near-wall structure obeys the universal law of the wall and whose skin friction follows well-established empirical correlations.

viscous sublayer  u⁺ = y⁺    log law  u⁺ = (1/κ) ln y⁺ + B,  κ≈0.41, B≈5.0
skin friction  Cf(Rex) ≈ 0.0592 Rex−1/5 (Prandtl)  or  (2 log₁₀ Rex − 0.65)−2.3 (Schlichting)

What this gates in real work

Real marine flows are turbulent — this case is the full-scale friction gate. A wall-resolved k-ω SST boundary layer that lands on the universal law of the wall qualifies:

  • Ship & FPSO hull friction — full-scale Cf prediction and the ITTC/Schoenherr line comparisons behind every powering estimate.
  • Appendage and rudder RANS — the same turbulence model, wall treatment (y⁺ ≈ 0.1–0.3, no wall functions) and near-wall meshing used for lifting surfaces (see the NACA0012 case).
  • Current loads on hulls and jackets — drag coefficients used in mooring and DP capability studies inherit their credibility from exactly this near-wall physics.

2  Primary result — the law of the wall

This is the definitive turbulence check. With the near-wall mesh resolved to y⁺≈0.13 (viscous sublayer, no wall functions bridging the gap), the k–ω SST solution reproduces all three canonical layers — sublayer, log law (κ≈0.41, B≈5.0), and wake. A model that got the turbulent wall physics wrong could not collapse onto the universal profile.

The empirical turbulent correlations themselves scatter by 5–10% (Prandtl, Schlichting, White, Kármán–Schoenherr), and they assume a boundary layer turbulent from the leading edge; the computed layer has a short resolved transition region near the plate nose (visible as the larger deficit at low Rex, shrinking downstream). The self-consistent law-of-the-wall collapse above — using the solution's own friction velocity — is the stronger and more fundamental verification of the turbulence model; the skin-friction level agrees with the classical correlations to within their own spread.
